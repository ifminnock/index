What This Game Is
Six project managers from our world are transported to a fantasy realm where PM skills are literal superpowers — absent for a thousand years. They defend the city of Cornerstone, fix its broken governance, and negotiate with the elven empire. Three arcs: Arc 1 = Crisis Management (siege), Arc 2 = Change Management (guild politics), Arc 3 = Stakeholder Management (elven diplomacy). Target: PMI Alaska Chapter. Long-term ambition: sellable corporate team-building experience.

Chapter ICampaign Overview

Premise: Six project managers from our world are transported to a fantasy realm where PM skills have been absent for a thousand years. They are valued not as warriors or wizards — but as people who can make things work.

  • Target Audience: PMI Alaska Chapter members — 6 players
  • Tone: Humorous fish-out-of-water meets genuine stakes. PM thinking is a superpower. Story over mechanics.
  • Long-term ambition: Sellable corporate team-building experience.
Designer Philosophy

The skills that feel mundane in a conference room are extraordinary in a world where no one has ever heard of a risk register. A spreadsheet is sorcery. A stakeholder matrix is a war map. A retrospective is prophecy.

Rules exist to serve the story. When a player applies a real PM concept in a genuinely applicable way, the mechanics reward it.

Core Rule Zero: If a player uses a real PM concept, framework, or tool in a genuinely applicable way, the GM may grant automatic success or a +2 bonus without a roll — even if the rules don't explicitly cover it.

Design Note
The game works best when PM players recognize real professional patterns in the fiction. Every major NPC maps to a workplace archetype. Every arc maps to a PM discipline. Keep this legible.
Long-Term Vision
Session Zero → Arc 1 → Arc 2 → Arc 3. Each arc is a standalone challenge. A corporate team-building version could run Arc 1 as a single session experience.
Chapter IIThe World — Lore Overview

The Chartered Realm is a continent whose history has been shaped by the presence and absence of one thing: the ability to organize collective human effort. When humanity had PM knowledge, it built Cornerstone and the Great Compact. When it lost that knowledge in the Severance, everything since has been slow collapse managed with diminishing returns.

Cornerstone

A city of approximately forty thousand people built at the convergence of three river valleys. Named for the PM principle: the foundational piece everything depends on. Currently under partial siege by the Gurach Vel orcish confederation. King Charles Emberson Winterpeg the 33rd is preparing for his birthday.

The Severance

Seven hundred years ago, a human faction betrayed the Great Compact from within — opening the gates to a political and spiritual collapse that ended the age of organized governance. Elves were exiled. Dwarves withdrew. The capacity for coordinated human effort was either destroyed or hidden as a consequence — accounts differ. The Severance is the original wound. Everything in the campaign is downstream of it.

The PM Skills

In this world, PM knowledge is not merely professional training — it is a form of power that has been absent so long it reads as supernatural. A stakeholder matrix is a war map. A risk register is prophecy. The team's arrival is not incidental. Whether they were summoned, fell through, or were pulled deliberately is a question the campaign may or may not answer.

World-Build Hook
The Compact of Guilds failed 400 years ago for documented reasons. If those records survive, the team can reference them in Arc 2: "Here's what broke the last attempt. Here's our mitigation plan." That is extremely PM.
World-Build Hook
What happened to Varath — the first human coastal settlement, now destroyed? A ruined city with early PM governance records could be an Arc 2 Discovery mission. The team literally excavates an ancient project post-mortem.
Chapter IIIArc Structure

Three arcs. Each represents a distinct PM discipline and a distinct type of challenge. The team enters Arc 1 with no reputation and no resources. By Arc 3 they are operating at the level of a recognized regional power.

Arc 1 — The Siege (Crisis Management)

Situation: The Gurach Vel orcish confederation has laid siege to Cornerstone. King Charles Emberson Winterpeg the 33rd is more concerned with his upcoming 33rd birthday than the military situation. The team arrives and must stabilize the crisis.

Core PM discipline: Crisis management. Triage. Stakeholder communication under pressure. Keeping the clock from running out.

Arc 1 Resolution Paths
  • Negotiated settlement: Broker a ceasefire and territorial agreement over the mines. Droga Vorak will negotiate if convinced it's advantageous — not from weakness, from pragmatism. The team must make the case that a deal serves orcish interests better than a siege. This path requires acknowledging the legitimacy of the territorial grievance.
  • Military action: Support the defense, coordinate Cornerstone's resources, and outlast or repel the siege. Requires Construction and Government skill synergies. Does not resolve the underlying land dispute — sets up future conflict.
  • Internal advocacy: The team discovers the mines genuinely are on ancestral orcish land. They can advocate internally — to the King, the guilds — for the orcs' legitimate claim. This is the hardest path politically and the most rewarding narratively. Enemies will be made. The right outcome may cost something real.

Note on the orcish framing: The non-human people whose land is being taken are not the obstacle to be managed around. Their grievance is legitimate. The team must decide whether to solve the political problem or the ethical one — and whether those are the same thing.

Arc 1 specific scenes, decision points, and encounter structure needed. Key beats: arrival scene, first Vorak contact, King birthday scene (comic relief / political disaster), negotiation or battle climax.
Arc 2 — The Guild Deadlock (Change Management)

Situation: The siege is resolved. Cornerstone needs functioning governance. The four guilds must be brought into a coherent framework. Wilhelm Klaus is actively working to prevent it. The team has to build something that outlasts their presence.

Core PM discipline: Change management. Stakeholder alignment. Organizational design. Managing resistance that won't announce itself.

The Klaus/Nicole dynamic: Klaus never gets his hands dirty. He is conflict-avoidant — he schemes, he funds, he positions. Nicole Fennigan does the operational betrayal work: undermining Yuki Wei's authority in the Craftsmen's Guild while quietly negotiating cross-guild consolidation with Klaus. The team will identify Nicole as the threat first. Klaus remains obscured until late Arc 2.

Arc 2 specific scenes needed. Key beats: first guild council, Nicole's first move, discovery of the Merchants' old trade records (Severance evidence), Klaus revealed, governance framework vote.
Arc 3 — The Emissary (Stakeholder Management)

Situation: Ambassador Sylvari Aethon arrives from Aethis Lor. The elves have been watching. They have grievances a thousand years old and questions about whether what the team has built can survive without them. This arc is diplomacy at civilizational scale.

Core PM discipline: Stakeholder management. Long-term relationship building. Managing parties with legitimately incompatible histories.

Aethon framing: Aethon's grief is about The Severance — a betrayal by humans, seven hundred years of exile, and the slow watch as Cornerstone failed to live up to what it once was. This is not about a dark external enemy. It is about whether humans can be trusted again. The team is the evidence either way.

Arc 3 scenes, Aethon personal history, elven political structure, cross-cultural negotiation mechanics, and connection to Letters from the Field author all needed.
Arc Design Note
Each arc should have a "what breaks if the team does nothing" answer ready. Siege: Cornerstone falls. Guild deadlock: Klaus consolidates power and the governance window closes. Elven diplomacy: Aethis Lor withdraws and the dark forces Aethon mentioned gain ground unopposed.
World-Build Hook
The Compact of Guilds failed 400 years ago. If those records exist in the Merchants' archives, the team can learn exactly how Klaus-style consolidation destroyed governance last time. History as project post-mortem.

The internal advocacy path in Arc 1 is the hardest and most interesting resolution. Build at least one scene that makes this path visible to players who might not think to pursue it.
Chapter IVMaking Tests

Tests are how uncertainty gets resolved at the table. Not every action needs a test — only those with genuine stakes, real opposition, or meaningful uncertainty.

The Resolution Sequence

1. Describe your action. Say what you're doing and how.

2. GM names the relevant stat. Which trait category does this call on? (Conflict, Evidence, or Decision)

3. Roll 2d6. This is always the base.

4. Check your dominant trait. If the stat called matches a trait that defines your character — not a skill you have, but something you genuinely are — add a d4 to the roll.

5. Add skill bonuses. If a relevant skill applies, add +1 (or +2 for SME passives).

6. Read the total.

TotalResultWhat It Means
10+Full successYou get what you were going for.
7–9Partial successYou succeed, but with a complication, cost, or condition.
6–FailureIt doesn't work. The story gets harder — not over.
Dominant Traits — The d4

Stats in this game are not numbers. They are identity descriptors. You don't have Assertiveness at level 3 — you are assertive, and that is simply true about your character. If it's true, you roll the d4. If it isn't, you don't.

Each character has three stat slots, each a binary choice. Mark one per slot at character creation. That mark doesn't change mid-scene — it's who you are, not a mode you switch into.

The d4 is the mechanical expression of identity. It adds tactile variety to the roll, rewards character specificity, and creates moments where the right person for the job rolls noticeably different from the wrong one. A d6 is available as an upgrade path for signature traits — see Advancement.

Example Test

The team needs to convince Droga Vorak to pause the siege for a negotiation window. Vorak is Hostile. One player declares: "I'm going to tell Vorak directly that his tactical position is strong — and that's exactly why he can afford to negotiate. I'm not asking him to back down, I'm reframing this as his choice."

GM calls Conflict. The player is Assertive (marked) — rolls 2d6 + d4. Gets 3+5+3 = 11. Full success. Vorak doesn't soften — but he listens. The negotiation opens.

A different player tries the same approach but is Collaborative, not Assertive. Same Conflict stat, no d4. Rolls 2d6, gets 7. Partial — Vorak agrees to one exchange but sets a condition.

When Not to Roll

Do not roll for: automatic success when clever PM tactics apply; busywork; established facts; actions with no failure state worth dramatizing.

Fortune Check: When external circumstances intervene — weather, timing, a third party's unpredictable action. This roll represents the world, not a player's competence. Name it explicitly before rolling. Advancement skills cannot reroll a Fortune Check.

GM Failure Rule: When a roll fails, the GM must answer: "What gets harder? What new decision must now be made?" If nothing changes, it wasn't worth rolling.

Design Note — The d4
The d4 is physically weird and fun. Players notice when they pick it up. The upgrade path to d6 for a signature trait (via Advancement) gives long-term players a meaningful marker of character development.

Chapter VDominant Traits (Stats)

Three stat slots. Each is a binary choice — you mark one, and that is who your character is. Marked traits add a d4 when the relevant stat is called in a test.

Slot I — Conflict · How you engage with opposition
Assertive

You push, hold ground, and direct. When things need to move, you move them. Add d4 to tests involving pressure, direction, or holding a position under opposition.

Collaborative

You build consensus and shared ownership. When things need to hold together, you hold them. Add d4 to tests involving alignment, trust-building, or getting others to choose the same direction.

Slot II — Evidence · How you process information
Data-Minded

You trust documentation, evidence, and analysis. Add d4 to tests where research, records, or structured reasoning support your position.

Instinct-Driven

You read patterns and trust field judgment over documentation. Add d4 to tests requiring rapid assessment, reading a room, or acting on incomplete information correctly.

Slot III — Decision · How you act under pressure
Pragmatic

You cut through noise. You identify the critical path and pursue it without sentiment. Add d4 to tests making hard choices, finding efficient solutions, or staying functional when things go sideways.

Conviction

You make others believe. You commit publicly and pull people into your momentum. Add d4 to tests persuading others to commit, backing decisions publicly, or holding a vision under pressure.

Design Check
Each trait should have a featured scene per arc where it clearly shines. Data-Minded: Arc 2 governance design. Instinct-Driven: Arc 1 siege chaos. Collaborative: Arc 3 diplomacy. Build deliberately.
Open — Advancement
Signature trait upgrade (d4 → d6) is the primary long-term mechanical reward for a trait. Formalize when Advancement system is built.
Chapter VIJobs (Character Classes)

Each job grants a passive ability — a narratively powerful truth about how you see the world. Players choose a job and an industry. The combination defines your character's approach.

```
Project Manager
Passive: Critical Eye

You identify the single point of failure. Designate one task or resource as "linchpin" — if it fails, everyone at the table understands the cascading consequences before they happen.

Program Manager
Passive: Orchestration

You see how multiple initiatives interconnect. When making decisions involving 3+ moving pieces, gain narrative clarity on dependencies and interactions.

Portfolio Manager
Passive: Strategic Horizon

You see which initiatives should exist and which shouldn't. Once per arc, you may formally recommend that a project, plan, or effort be stopped — and the GM must honestly assess whether that recommendation is correct and what stopping it would free up. Other characters can ignore your recommendation. They cannot ignore its accuracy.

Scrum Master
Passive: Retrospective Clarity

After each failed attempt, name one specific thing that went wrong and how to avoid it. Subsequent attempts targeting that lesson get +1. The bonus persists until used once, then resets.

Product Owner
Passive: Roadmap Vision

You see the endpoint. Ask "If we succeed, what does the city or system look like in three months?" and the GM answers honestly.

Change Manager
Passive: Resistance Mapping

You see not just who is resisting, but why — and what it would actually cost them to change. Once per scene: ask the GM "What is this NPC or faction's specific resistance to this change, and what would genuinely address it?" The GM answers honestly.

Intern
Passive: Master of None

No traditional passive. Instead, pick skills from any industry and start with +1 additional skill at character creation. The generalist who covers any gap — this is not a joke option.

Subject Matter Expert
Passive: Domain Authority

Double all roll bonuses from your industry skill. If your skill grants +1, you roll at +2. Simple, universal, scales with your industry choice.

```

Chapter VIIIScene Structure

Three scene types cover the full range of play. Each has different pacing, different roll triggers, and different GM responsibilities.

Scene Type I — Crisis Scene

High stakes, time pressure, round-robin turns. Used for sieges, emergency decisions, system failures, sudden political ruptures.

1. Setup (GM): Describe crisis and clock. "The breach opens in 30 minutes."

2. Round-Robin: Strict timer — 5 min/player routine, 2 min/player maximum urgency (GM's call). One clear action or decision per turn.

3. GM Adjudication (1–2 min): Which actions land? What needs a roll? New state?

4. Repeat — typically 2–4 rounds.

Scene Type II — Negotiation Scene

Free-form dialogue with structured stakes. No timer — GM tracks NPC disposition and can close the scene if momentum stalls.

Disposition Track: GM privately notes NPC starting position: Hostile / Skeptical / Neutral / Receptive / Committed. Player actions move this track.

Rolls: Partial (7–9) moves NPC one step, adds condition. Full (10+) moves further or drops conditions. Failure (6–) moves wrong direction.

Scene Type III — Discovery Scene

Exploratory and investigative. No timer, no negotiation target. Goal: leave knowing something that changes a decision.

The Discovery Rule: Every Discovery Scene yields at least one piece of information that changes a decision the team thought they had already made. If nothing learned affects future choices, the scene wasn't necessary.

Rolls: Only when information is hidden, guarded, or dangerous to obtain.

When to Roll

Roll only for: genuine uncertainty, real risk, active opposition, or a Fortune Check.

Fortune Check: External circumstances intervene — weather, timing, a third party's unpredictable action. This roll represents the world, not the player's competence. Always name it explicitly.

Timer Note
6 players at 5 min/turn = 30 min/round before adjudication. Intentional for deliberate scenes. 2-min cap is the release valve — use freely. Consider a visible timer at the table.
World-Build Hook
The Disposition Track maps directly to how elven NPCs run in Arc 3. Aethon starts at Skeptical minimum. Build NPC-specific starting dispositions for all major recurring characters.

```
Archival Record: The Scepter Incident

Recovered from the personal journal of Mira Aldstone, Team Scribe. Third Year, Season of the Long Rain.

It began, as most disasters do in Cornerstone, with Edric.

"The king," Edric announced at breakfast, with the calm of a man who had already decided, "is incompetent. His scepter is the symbol of his authority. If I held it, I would hold Cornerstone."

The table went quiet. Aldric set down his quill. Yenna looked at the ceiling. I wrote oh no in the margin of my notes, which I stand by as accurate documentation.

"You want to steal the king's scepter," said Aldric, "and declare yourself ruler of a city of forty thousand people who do not know you."

"I want to borrow it. Diplomatically."

"A vote," said Yenna. "All opposed?" Four hands. Edric's stayed down. The motion failed, four to one.

Edric nodded pleasantly and excused himself from breakfast.

Designer's Note: James (playing Edric) declared his intent to proceed despite the vote. The GM called for a Conviction test — Edric's Slot III trait.

James rolled a 9. Partial success. The court didn't stop him — but they remembered.

He got the scepter. Suffice to say: Edric walked into the throne room, spoke for eleven minutes without interruption, and walked out holding a gold rod the length of his arm while three advisors applauded and the king looked like a man watching a carriage roll slowly into a river.

For approximately four hours, Edric was the most famous man in Cornerstone. Then the Merchants' Guild sent a letter. Then the Scholars' Guild sent a letter. Then the Soldiers' Guild sent a man — not a letter, a man — who stood in our doorway and said nothing, which was somehow worse.

Designer's Note: The GM assessed the fallout. Edric's Conviction test succeeded at 9 — partial. The team's collective Renown absorbed the consequence.

Team Renown: −1. Merchants' Guild: Skeptical → Hostile. King's personal trust: reduced.

The scepter was returned. The lesson: you can be right and still cost everyone around you something. Edric was not wrong about the king. He was wrong about the method, the timing, and the assumption that his Conviction was stronger than the team's collective reputation.

— Mira Aldstone, Team Scribe
```

Chapter XThe Four Guilds of Cornerstone

Cornerstone's guilds were originally designed to organize civic life. Over a thousand years without functional governance, they became something else: parallel power structures with territorial interests and deep mutual suspicion. They do not cooperate. They coexist, barely.

The team's arrival in Arc 2 threatens this balance. The guilds will each attempt to use the team for their own ends before the team can figure out how to use the guilds for the city's.

```
The Craftsmen's Guild — The Honest Workers

What they do: Builders, engineers, stonemasons, carpenters. The people who actually make things. They built the walls that held during the siege.

Political position: Most sympathetic, least powerful. They have labor but not capital. Routinely underpaid by the Merchants' Guild and overcharged by the Soldiers' Guild.

Relationship to team: Immediate allies. Construction industry skill resonates naturally with their world. Trust is fast here — and most costly to break.

Hidden dynamic: Yuki Wei is old and tired. Nicole Fennigan is her deputy and is actively undermining her while negotiating secretly with Wilhelm Klaus. The Craftsmen's Guild may not be as unified as it appears. The team will likely not see this coming unless they're paying close attention to internal faction dynamics.

Arc 2 role: Most likely first to accept the team's governance framework. Their buy-in gives the process legitimacy with the population.

The Merchants' Guild — The Pragmatists

What they do: Trade, supply chains, moneylending, information brokerage. During the siege, they were the only faction with a functional supply chain — because they had been quietly maintaining one for their own purposes for decades.

Political position: Most flexible, most transactional. They profit from the chaos. A functional governance framework might reduce their leverage.

Relationship to team: Hostile after the Scepter Incident. Cautiously interested in the team's competence; actively opposed to their governance agenda.

The Klaus operation: Wilhelm Klaus has no interest in open conflict. He schemes. His current play: use Nicole Fennigan to destabilize Yuki Wei's leadership in the Craftsmen's Guild, then position himself to broker a monopolistic merger that gives the Merchants' Guild effective control of both trade and production. This is not a violent plan. It is a project plan — with better resources than the team has.

Hidden dynamic: The Merchants' Guild records contain evidence of who funded the political betrayal that caused the Severance. They don't know they know — it's buried in their oldest trade records. A Research PM with the right advancement skill might find it.

Arc 2 role: The swing vote. Whichever governance framework wins Merchants' support will pass. They will extract a price for that support.

The Scholars' Guild — The Gate-Keepers

What they do: Knowledge, arcane research, education (for a fee), ritual services. They have a monopoly on literacy in large portions of the city.

Political position: Deeply threatened by the team. The Scholars' Guild has survived a thousand years by being the only people in the room who could analyze complex problems. The team does this better, faster, and without charging for it.
```

Appendix AA Brief History of the Chartered Realm

The following is the commonly accepted human version of history — filtered through a thousand years of selective record-keeping. Elves and dwarves tell it differently. Orcs tell it differently still.

The Deep Past

The Great Wyrms hold dominion. Orcish clans roam the Central Plains freely. The elven Dominion exists but has no interest in the rest of the continent. Humans are nomadic.

~1,200 years ago

First human settlements. A coastal trading post grows into Varath — now long destroyed. Humans begin expanding inland, following orcish trade routes. This is the beginning of the territorial encroachment.

~800 years ago

The Age of the Great Compact. Elves, dwarves, and humans build together. Cornerstone founded at the convergence of three river valleys — named for the PM principle of the foundational piece everything depends on. Peak of human PM knowledge.

~700 years ago

The Severance. A human faction betrays from within — opens the gates for reasons that remain politically contested. Elves exiled. Dwarves withdraw. The capacity for organized human effort is either destroyed or hidden as a consequence — accounts differ. The Great Compact collapses. Orcs lose their most significant diplomatic counterpart.

~400 years ago

The Compact of Guilds. Cornerstone's first post-Severance governance attempt — five guild masters agreeing to shared trade rules. Works for sixty years before collapsing into the First Guild War. The ghost of this failure is why Arc 2's Guild Deadlock hits so hard: everyone knows it has happened before.

~50 years ago

The King's Ascension. Current king's father unified Cornerstone through military force and genuine economic reform. The mines expand significantly during this period — directly into orcish territorial claims.

Campaign Present

The Gurach Vel siege begins. The team arrives. King Charles Emberson Winterpeg the 33rd is turning 33. Everything changes — or doesn't, depending on what they do.

The mine expansion 50 years ago is when the current territorial grievance became acute. This is documentable — there are records. A Discovery scene in the royal archives could find them. What happens when the team proves the orcish claim in writing?

What happened to Varath? A ruined city with early PM governance records could be an Arc 2 Discovery mission. The team literally excavates an ancient project post-mortem.

The Severance cost the orcs their most significant diplomatic counterpart (the elves). Clan Urroch oral tradition likely preserves the orcish account of the Severance. A Research PM engaging Clan Urroch could find a version of history that changes everything the team thinks they know.
